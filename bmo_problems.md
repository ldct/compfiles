# British Mathematical Olympiad (BMO) Problems, 2005–2025

Verbatim problem statements collected from the official BMOS archive at https://bmos.ukmt.org.uk/home/bmo.shtml. Each year section cites the two source PDFs (BMO1 and BMO2). Topic tags are assigned as one of: NumberTheory, Algebra, Combinatorics, Geometry, Inequality, FunctionalEquation.

Note: The "year" here is the year the round was held (BMO1 in late autumn of the previous calendar year, BMO2 in January of the named year). Example: "2005" refers to BMO1 1 Dec 2004 and BMO2 1 Feb 2005.

---

## 2005

Sources:
- BMO1: https://bmos.ukmt.org.uk/home/bmo1-2005.pdf
- BMO2: https://bmos.ukmt.org.uk/home/bmo2-2005.pdf

### Round 1 (BMO1 2004/5, 1 December 2004)

**Problem 1** (Algebra)
Each of Paul and Jenny has a whole number of pounds. He says to her: "If you give me £3, I will have n times as much as you". She says to him: "If you give me £n, I will have 3 times as much as you". Given that all these statements are true and that n is a positive integer, what are the possible values for n?

**Problem 2** (Geometry)
Let ABC be an acute-angled triangle, and let D, E be the feet of the perpendiculars from A, B to BC, CA respectively. Let P be the point where the line AD meets the semicircle constructed outwardly on BC, and Q be the point where the line BE meets the semicircle constructed outwardly on AC. Prove that CP = CQ.

**Problem 3** (Combinatorics)
Determine the least natural number n for which the following result holds: No matter how the elements of the set {1, 2, . . . , n} are coloured red or blue, there are integers x, y, z, w in the set (not necessarily distinct) of the same colour such that x + y + z = w.

**Problem 4** (NumberTheory)
Determine the least possible value of the largest term in an arithmetic progression of seven distinct primes.

**Problem 5** (Algebra)
Let S be a set of rational numbers with the following properties:
i) 1/2 ∈ S;
ii) If x ∈ S, then both 1/(x+1) ∈ S and x/(x+1) ∈ S.
Prove that S contains all rational numbers in the interval 0 < x < 1.

(Note: The BMO1 2004/5 paper has 5 problems rather than 6.)

### Round 2 (BMO2 2005, 1 February 2005)

**Problem 1** (NumberTheory)
The integer N is positive. There are exactly 2005 ordered pairs (x, y) of positive integers satisfying 1/x + 1/y = 1/N. Prove that N is a perfect square.

**Problem 2** (Geometry)
In triangle ABC, ∠BAC = 120°. Let the angle bisectors of angles A, B and C meet the opposite sides in D, E and F respectively. Prove that the circle on diameter EF passes through D.

**Problem 3** (Inequality)
Let a, b, c be positive real numbers. Prove that
(a/b + b/c + c/a)² ≥ (a + b + c)(1/a + 1/b + 1/c).

**Problem 4** (Combinatorics)
Let X = {A₁, A₂, . . . , Aₙ} be a set of distinct 3-element subsets of {1, 2, . . . , 36} such that
i) Aᵢ and Aⱼ have non-empty intersection for every i, j.
ii) The intersection of all the elements of X is the empty set.
Show that n ≤ 100. How many such sets X are there when n = 100?

## 2006

Sources:
- BMO1: https://bmos.ukmt.org.uk/home/bmo1-2006.pdf
- BMO2: https://bmos.ukmt.org.uk/home/bmo2-2006.pdf

### Round 1 (BMO1 2005/6, 30 November 2005)

**Problem 1** (NumberTheory)
Let n be an integer greater than 6. Prove that if n − 1 and n + 1 are both prime, then n²(n² + 16) is divisible by 720. Is the converse true?

**Problem 2** (Combinatorics)
Adrian teaches a class of six pairs of twins. He wishes to set up teams for a quiz, but wants to avoid putting any pair of twins into the same team. Subject to this condition:
i) In how many ways can he split them into two teams of six?
ii) In how many ways can he split them into three teams of four?

**Problem 3** (Geometry)
In the cyclic quadrilateral ABCD, the diagonal AC bisects the angle DAB. The side AD is extended beyond D to a point E. Show that CE = CA if and only if DE = AB.

**Problem 4** (Combinatorics)
The equilateral triangle ABC has sides of integer length N. The triangle is completely divided (by drawing lines parallel to the sides of the triangle) into equilateral triangular cells of side length 1. A continuous route is chosen, starting inside the cell with vertex A and always crossing from one cell to another through an edge shared by the two cells. No cell is visited more than once. Find, with proof, the greatest number of cells which can be visited.

**Problem 5** (Geometry)
Let G be a convex quadrilateral. Show that there is a point X in the plane of G with the property that every straight line through X divides G into two regions of equal area if and only if G is a parallelogram.

**Problem 6** (Combinatorics)
Let T be a set of 2005 coplanar points with no three collinear. Show that, for any of the 2005 points, the number of triangles it lies strictly within, whose vertices are points in T, is even.

### Round 2 (BMO2 2005/6, 31 January 2006)

**Problem 1** (Algebra)
Find the minimum possible value of x² + y² given that x and y are real numbers satisfying xy(x² − y²) = x² + y² and x ≠ 0.

**Problem 2** (NumberTheory)
Let x and y be positive integers with no prime factors larger than 5. Find all such x and y which satisfy x² − y² = 2ᵏ for some non-negative integer k.

**Problem 3** (Geometry)
Let ABC be a triangle with AC > AB. The point X lies on the side BA extended through A, and the point Y lies on the side CA in such a way that BX = CA and CY = BA. The line XY meets the perpendicular bisector of side BC at P. Show that ∠BPC + ∠BAC = 180°.

**Problem 4** (Combinatorics)
An exam consisting of six questions is sat by 2006 children. Each question is marked either right or wrong. Any three children have right answers to at least five of the six questions between them. Let N be the total number of right answers achieved by all the children (i.e. the total number of questions solved by child 1 + the total solved by child 2 + · · · + the total solved by child 2006). Find the least possible value of N.

## 2007

Sources:
- BMO1: https://bmos.ukmt.org.uk/home/bmo1-2007.pdf
- BMO2: https://bmos.ukmt.org.uk/home/bmo2-2007.pdf

### Round 1 (BMO1 2006/7, 1 December 2006)

**Problem 1** (NumberTheory)
Find four prime numbers less than 100 which are factors of 3³² − 2³².

**Problem 2** (Geometry)
In the convex quadrilateral ABCD, points M, N lie on the side AB such that AM = MN = NB, and points P, Q lie on the side CD such that CP = PQ = QD. Prove that Area of AMCP = Area of MNPQ = (1/3) · Area of ABCD.

**Problem 3** (Combinatorics)
The number 916238457 is an example of a nine-digit number which contains each of the digits 1 to 9 exactly once. It also has the property that the digits 1 to 5 occur in their natural order, while the digits 1 to 6 do not. How many such numbers are there?

**Problem 4** (Geometry)
Two touching circles S and T share a common tangent which meets S at A and T at B. Let AP be a diameter of S and let the tangent from P to T touch it at Q. Show that AP = PQ.

**Problem 5** (Inequality)
For positive real numbers a, b, c, prove that
(a² + b²)² ≥ (a + b + c)(a + b − c)(b + c − a)(c + a − b).

**Problem 6** (NumberTheory)
Let n be an integer. Show that, if 2 + 2√(1 + 12n²) is an integer, then it is a perfect square.

### Round 2 (BMO2 2006/7, 30 January 2007)

**Problem 1** (NumberTheory)
Triangle ABC has integer-length sides, and AC = 2007. The internal bisector of ∠BAC meets BC at D. Given that AB = CD, determine AB and BC.

**Problem 2** (NumberTheory)
Show that there are infinitely many pairs of positive integers (m, n) such that (m+1)/n + (n+1)/m is a positive integer.

**Problem 3** (Geometry)
Let ABC be an acute-angled triangle with AB > AC and ∠BAC = 60°. Denote the circumcentre by O and the orthocentre by H and let OH meet AB at P and AC at Q. Prove that PO = HQ.

**Problem 4** (Combinatorics)
In the land of Hexagonia, the six cities are connected by a rail network such that there is a direct rail line connecting each pair of cities. On Sundays, some lines may be closed for repair. The passengers' rail charter stipulates that any city must be accessible by rail from any other (not necessarily directly) at all times. In how many different ways can some of the lines be closed subject to this condition?

## 2008

Sources:
- BMO1: https://bmos.ukmt.org.uk/home/bmo1-2008.pdf
- BMO2: https://bmos.ukmt.org.uk/home/bmo2-2008.pdf

### Round 1 (BMO1 2007/8, 30 November 2007)

**Problem 1** (Algebra)
Find the value of (1⁴ + 2007⁴ + 2008⁴) / (1² + 2007² + 2008²).

**Problem 2** (NumberTheory)
Find all solutions in positive integers x, y, z to the simultaneous equations
x + y − z = 12
x² + y² − z² = 12.

**Problem 3** (Geometry)
Let ABC be a triangle, with an obtuse angle at A. Let Q be a point (other than A, B or C) on the circumcircle of the triangle, on the same side of chord BC as A, and let P be the other end of the diameter through Q. Let V and W be the feet of the perpendiculars from Q onto CA and AB respectively. Prove that the triangles PBC and AWV are similar. [Note: the circumcircle of the triangle ABC is the circle which passes through the vertices A, B and C.]

**Problem 4** (Combinatorics)
Let S be a subset of the set of numbers {1, 2, 3, ..., 2008} which consists of 756 distinct numbers. Show that there are two distinct elements a, b of S such that a + b is divisible by 8.

**Problem 5** (Geometry)
Let P be an internal point of triangle ABC. The line through P parallel to AB meets BC at L, the line through P parallel to BC meets CA at M, and the line through P parallel to CA meets AB at N. Prove that
(BL/LC) × (CM/MA) × (AN/NB) ≤ 1/8
and locate the position of P in triangle ABC when equality holds.

**Problem 6** (NumberTheory)
The function f is defined on the set of positive integers by f(1) = 1, f(2n) = 2f(n), and nf(2n + 1) = (2n + 1)(f(n) + n) for all n ≥ 1.
i) Prove that f(n) is always an integer.
ii) For how many positive integers less than 2007 is f(n) = 2n?

### Round 2 (BMO2 2007/8, 31 January 2008)

**Problem 1** (Algebra)
Find the minimum value of x² + y² + z² where x, y, z are real numbers such that x³ + y³ + z³ − 3xyz = 1.

**Problem 2** (Geometry)
Let triangle ABC have incentre I and circumcentre O. Suppose that ∠AIO = 90° and ∠CIO = 45°. Find the ratio AB : BC : CA.

**Problem 3** (Combinatorics)
Adrian has drawn a circle in the xy-plane whose radius is a positive integer at most 2008. The origin lies somewhere inside the circle. You are allowed to ask him questions of the form "Is the point (x, y) inside your circle?" After each question he will answer truthfully "yes" or "no". Show that it is always possible to deduce the radius of the circle after at most sixty questions. [Note: Any point which lies exactly on the circle may be considered to lie inside the circle.]

**Problem 4** (NumberTheory)
Prove that there are infinitely many pairs of distinct positive integers x, y such that x² + y³ is divisible by x³ + y².

## 2009

Sources:
- BMO1: https://bmos.ukmt.org.uk/home/bmo1-2009.pdf
- BMO2: https://bmos.ukmt.org.uk/home/bmo2-2009.pdf

### Round 1 (BMO1 2008/9, 4 December 2008)

**Problem 1** (Combinatorics)
Consider a standard 8 × 8 chessboard consisting of 64 small squares coloured in the usual pattern, so 32 are black and 32 are white. A zig-zag path across the board is a collection of eight white squares, one in each row, which meet at their corners. How many zig-zag paths are there?

**Problem 2** (Algebra)
Find all real values of x, y and z such that (x + 1)yz = 12, (y + 1)zx = 4 and (z + 1)xy = 4.

**Problem 3** (Geometry)
Let ABPC be a parallelogram such that ABC is an acute-angled triangle. The circumcircle of triangle ABC meets the line CP again at Q. Prove that PQ = AC if, and only if, ∠BAC = 60°. The circumcircle of a triangle is the circle which passes through its vertices.

**Problem 4** (NumberTheory)
Find all positive integers n such that both n + 2008 divides n² + 2008 and n + 2009 divides n² + 2009.

**Problem 5** (Algebra)
Determine the sequences a₀, a₁, a₂, . . . which satisfy all of the following conditions:
a) a_{n+1} = 2a_n² − 1 for every integer n ≥ 0,
b) a₀ is a rational number and
c) aᵢ = aⱼ for some i, j with i ≠ j.

**Problem 6** (Inequality)
The obtuse-angled triangle ABC has sides of length a, b and c opposite the angles ∠A, ∠B and ∠C respectively. Prove that
a³ cos A + b³ cos B + c³ cos C < abc.

### Round 2 (BMO2 2008/9, 29 January 2009)

**Problem 1** (NumberTheory)
Find all solutions in non-negative integers a, b to √a + √b = √2009.

**Problem 2** (Geometry)
Let ABC be an acute-angled triangle with ∠B = ∠C. Let the circumcentre be O and the orthocentre be H. Prove that the centre of the circle BOH lies on the line AB. The circumcentre of a triangle is the centre of its circumcircle. The orthocentre of a triangle is the point where its three altitudes meet.

**Problem 3** (FunctionalEquation)
Find all functions f from the real numbers to the real numbers which satisfy f(x³) + f(y³) = (x + y)(f(x²) + f(y²) − f(xy)) for all real numbers x and y.

**Problem 4** (Combinatorics)
Given a positive integer n, let b(n) denote the number of positive integers whose binary representations occur as blocks of consecutive integers in the binary expansion of n. For example b(13) = 6 because 13 = 1101₂, which contains as consecutive blocks the binary representations of 13 = 1101₂, 6 = 110₂, 5 = 101₂, 3 = 11₂, 2 = 10₂ and 1 = 1₂. Show that if n ≤ 2500, then b(n) ≤ 39, and determine the values of n for which equality holds.

## 2010

Sources:
- BMO1: https://bmos.ukmt.org.uk/home/bmo1-2010.pdf
- BMO2: https://bmos.ukmt.org.uk/home/bmo2-2010.pdf

### Round 1 (BMO1 2009/10, 3 December 2009)

**Problem 1** (NumberTheory)
Find all integers x, y and z such that x² + y² + z² = 2(yz + 1) and x + y + z = 4018.

**Problem 2** (Geometry)
Points A, B, C, D and E lie, in that order, on a circle and the lines AB and ED are parallel. Prove that ∠ABC = 90° if, and only if, AC² = BD² + CE².

**Problem 3** (Combinatorics)
Isaac attempts all six questions on an Olympiad paper in order. Each question is marked on a scale from 0 to 10. He never scores more in a later question than in any earlier question. How many different possible sequences of six marks can he achieve?

**Problem 4** (Geometry)
Two circles, of different radius, with centres at B and C, touch externally at A. A common tangent, not through A, touches the first circle at D and the second at E. The line through A which is perpendicular to DE and the perpendicular bisector of BC meet at F. Prove that BC = 2AF.

**Problem 5** (FunctionalEquation)
Find all functions f, defined on the real numbers and taking real values, which satisfy the equation f(x)f(y) = f(x + y) + xy for all real numbers x and y.

**Problem 6** (NumberTheory)
Long John Silverman has captured a treasure map from Adam McBones. Adam has buried the treasure at the point (x, y) with integer co-ordinates (not necessarily positive). He has indicated on the map the values of x² + y and x + y², and these numbers are distinct. Prove that Long John has to dig only in one place to find the treasure.

### Round 2 (BMO2 2009/10, 28 January 2010)

**Problem 1** (Combinatorics)
There are 2010^{2010} children at a mathematics camp. Each has at most three friends at the camp, and if A is friends with B, then B is friends with A. The camp leader would like to line the children up so that there are at most 2010 children between any pair of friends. Is it always possible to do this?

**Problem 2** (Geometry)
In triangle ABC the centroid is G and D is the midpoint of CA. The line through G parallel to BC meets AB at E. Prove that ∠AEC = ∠DGC if, and only if, ∠ACB = 90°.

**Problem 3** (NumberTheory)
The integer x is at least 3 and n = x⁶ − 1. Let p be a prime and k be a positive integer such that p^k is a factor of n. Show that p^{3k} < 8n.

**Problem 4** (Inequality)
Prove that, for all positive real numbers x, y and z, 4(x + y + z)³ > 27(x²y + y²z + z²x).

## 2011

Sources:
- BMO1: https://bmos.ukmt.org.uk/home/bmo1-2011.pdf
- BMO2: https://bmos.ukmt.org.uk/home/bmo2-2011.pdf

### Round 1 (BMO1 2010/11, 2 December 2010)

**Problem 1** (Algebra)
One number is removed from the set of integers from 1 to n. The average of the remaining numbers is 40¾. Which integer was removed?

**Problem 2** (Algebra)
Let s be an integer greater than 6. A solid cube of side s has a square hole of side x < 6 drilled directly through from one face to the opposite face (so the drill removes a cuboid). The volume of the remaining solid is numerically equal to the total surface area of the remaining solid. Determine all possible integer values of x.

**Problem 3** (Geometry)
Let ABC be a triangle with ∠CAB a right-angle. The point L lies on the side BC between B and C. The circle ABL meets the line AC again at M and the circle CAL meets the line AB again at N. Prove that L, M and N lie on a straight line.

**Problem 4** (Combinatorics)
Isaac has a large supply of counters, and places one in each of the 1 × 1 squares of an 8 × 8 chessboard. Each counter is either red, white or blue. A particular pattern of coloured counters is called an arrangement. Determine whether there are more arrangements which contain an even number of red counters or more arrangements which contain an odd number of red counters. Note that 0 is an even number.

**Problem 5** (Geometry)
Circles S₁ and S₂ meet at L and M. Let P be a point on S₂. Let PL and PM meet S₁ again at Q and R respectively. The lines QM and RL meet at K. Show that, as P varies on S₂, K lies on a fixed circle.

**Problem 6** (Inequality)
Let a, b and c be the lengths of the sides of a triangle. Suppose that ab + bc + ca = 1. Show that (a + 1)(b + 1)(c + 1) < 4.

### Round 2 (BMO2 2010/11, 27 January 2011)

**Problem 1** (Geometry)
Let ABC be a triangle and X be a point inside the triangle. The lines AX, BX and CX meet the circle ABC again at P, Q and R respectively. Choose a point U on XP which is between X and P. Suppose that the lines through U which are parallel to AB and CA meet XQ and XR at points V and W respectively. Prove that the points R, W, V and Q lie on a circle.

**Problem 2** (NumberTheory)
Find all positive integers x and y such that x + y + 1 divides 2xy and x + y − 1 divides x² + y² − 1.

**Problem 3** (NumberTheory)
The function f is defined on the positive integers as follows:
f(1) = 1;
f(2n) = f(n) if n is even;
f(2n) = 2f(n) if n is odd;
f(2n + 1) = 2f(n) + 1 if n is even;
f(2n + 1) = f(n) if n is odd.
Find the number of positive integers n which are less than 2011 and have the property that f(n) = f(2011).

**Problem 4** (Combinatorics)
Let G be the set of points (x, y) in the plane such that x and y are integers in the range 1 ≤ x, y ≤ 2011. A subset S of G is said to be parallelogram-free if there is no proper parallelogram with all its vertices in S. Determine the largest possible size of a parallelogram-free subset of G. Note that a proper parallelogram is one where its vertices do not all lie on the same line.

## 2012

Sources:
- BMO1: https://bmos.ukmt.org.uk/home/bmo1-2012.pdf
- BMO2: https://bmos.ukmt.org.uk/home/bmo2-2012.pdf

### Round 1 (BMO1 2011/12, 2 December 2011)

**Problem 1** (NumberTheory)
Find all (positive or negative) integers n for which n² + 20n + 11 is a perfect square. Remember that you must justify that you have found them all.

**Problem 2** (Combinatorics)
Consider the numbers 1, 2, . . . , n. Find, in terms of n, the largest integer t such that these numbers can be arranged in a row so that all consecutive terms differ by at least t.

**Problem 3** (Geometry)
Consider a circle S. The point P lies outside S and a line is drawn through P, cutting S at distinct points X and Y. Circles S₁ and S₂ are drawn through P which are tangent to S at X and Y respectively. Prove that the difference of the radii of S₁ and S₂ is independent of the positions of P, X and Y.

**Problem 4** (Combinatorics)
Initially there are m balls in one bag, and n in the other, where m, n > 0. Two different operations are allowed:
a) Remove an equal number of balls from each bag;
b) Double the number of balls in one bag.
Is it always possible to empty both bags after a finite sequence of operations?
Operation b) is now replaced with
b′) Triple the number of balls in one bag.
Is it now always possible to empty both bags after a finite sequence of operations?

**Problem 5** (NumberTheory)
Prove that the product of four consecutive positive integers cannot be equal to the product of two consecutive positive integers.

**Problem 6** (Geometry)
Let ABC be an acute-angled triangle. The feet of the altitudes from A, B and C are D, E and F respectively. Prove that DE + DF ≤ BC and determine the triangles for which equality holds. The altitude from A is the line through A which is perpendicular to BC. The foot of this altitude is the point D where it meets BC. The other altitudes are similarly defined.

### Round 2 (BMO2 2011/12, 26 January 2012)

**Problem 1** (Geometry)
The diagonals AC and BD of a cyclic quadrilateral meet at E. The midpoints of the sides AB, BC, CD and DA are P, Q, R and S respectively. Prove that the circles EPS and EQR have the same radius.

**Problem 2** (NumberTheory)
A function f is defined on the positive integers by f(1) = 1 and, for n > 1,
f(n) = f(⌊(2n−1)/3⌋) + f(⌊2n/3⌋)
where ⌊x⌋ denotes the greatest integer less than or equal to x. Is it true that f(n) − f(n − 1) ≤ n for all n > 1?
[Here are some examples of the use of ⌊x⌋: ⌊π⌋ = 3, ⌊1729⌋ = 1729 and ⌊2012/1000⌋ = 2.]

**Problem 3** (Combinatorics)
The set of real numbers is split into two subsets which do not intersect. Prove that for each pair (m, n) of positive integers, there are real numbers x < y < z all in the same subset such that m(z − y) = n(y − x).

**Problem 4** (NumberTheory)
Show that there is a positive integer k with the following property: if a, b, c, d, e and f are integers and m is a divisor of a^n + b^n + c^n − d^n − e^n − f^n for all integers n in the range 1 ≤ n ≤ k, then m is a divisor of a^n + b^n + c^n − d^n − e^n − f^n for all positive integers n.

### Round 1 (BMO1 2012/13, 30 November 2012)

**Problem 1** (Combinatorics)
Isaac places some counters onto the squares of an 8 by 8 chessboard so that there is at most one counter in each of the 64 squares. Determine, with justification, the maximum number that he can place without having five or more counters in the same row, or in the same column, or on either of the two long diagonals.

**Problem 2** (Geometry)
Two circles S and T touch at X. They have a common tangent which meets S at A and T at B. The points A and B are different. Let AP be a diameter of S. Prove that B, X and P lie on a straight line.

**Problem 3** (Algebra)
Find all real numbers x, y and z which satisfy the simultaneous equations x² − 4y + 7 = 0, y² − 6z + 14 = 0 and z² − 2x − 7 = 0.

**Problem 4** (NumberTheory)
Find all positive integers n such that 12n − 119 and 75n − 539 are both perfect squares.

**Problem 5** (Geometry)
A triangle has sides of length at most 2, 3 and 4 respectively. Determine, with proof, the maximum possible area of the triangle.

**Problem 6** (Geometry)
Let ABC be a triangle. Let S be the circle through B tangent to CA at A and let T be the circle through C tangent to AB at A. The circles S and T intersect at A and D. Let E be the point where the line AD meets the circle ABC. Prove that D is the midpoint of AE.

### Round 2 (BMO2 2012/13, 31 January 2013)

**Problem 1** (NumberTheory)
Are there infinitely many pairs of positive integers (m, n) such that both m divides n² + 1 and n divides m² + 1?

**Problem 2** (Geometry)
The point P lies inside triangle ABC so that ∠ABP = ∠PCA. The point Q is such that PBQC is a parallelogram. Prove that ∠QAB = ∠CAP.

**Problem 3** (Combinatorics)
Consider the set of positive integers which, when written in binary, have exactly 2013 digits and more 0s than 1s. Let n be the number of such integers and let s be their sum. Prove that, when written in binary, n + s has more 0s than 1s.

**Problem 4** (NumberTheory)
Suppose that ABCD is a square and that P is a point which is on the circle inscribed in the square. Determine whether or not it is possible that PA, PB, PC, PD and AB are all integers.

## 2014

Sources:
- BMO1: https://bmos.ukmt.org.uk/home/bmo1-2014.pdf
- BMO2: https://bmos.ukmt.org.uk/home/bmo2-2014.pdf

### Round 1 (BMO1 2013/14, 29 November 2013)

**Problem 1** (Algebra)
Calculate the value of (2014⁴ + 4 × 2013⁴)/(2013² + 4027²) − (2012⁴ + 4 × 2013⁴)/(2013² + 4025²).

**Problem 2** (Geometry)
In the acute-angled triangle ABC, the foot of the perpendicular from B to CA is E. Let l be the tangent to the circle ABC at B. The foot of the perpendicular from C to l is F. Prove that EF is parallel to AB.

**Problem 3** (NumberTheory)
A number written in base 10 is a string of 3^{2013} digit 3s. No other digit appears. Find the highest power of 3 which divides this number.

**Problem 4** (Combinatorics)
Isaac is planning a nine-day holiday. Every day he will go surfing, or water skiing, or he will rest. On any given day he does just one of these three things. He never does different water-sports on consecutive days. How many schedules are possible for the holiday?

**Problem 5** (Geometry)
Let ABC be an equilateral triangle, and P be a point inside this triangle. Let D, E and F be the feet of the perpendiculars from P to the sides BC, CA and AB respectively. Prove that
a) AF + BD + CE = AE + BF + CD and
b) [APF] + [BPD] + [CPE] = [APE] + [BPF] + [CPD].
The area of triangle XYZ is denoted [XYZ].

**Problem 6** (Inequality)
The angles A, B and C of a triangle are measured in degrees, and the lengths of the opposite sides are a, b and c respectively. Prove that 60 ≤ (aA + bB + cC)/(a + b + c) < 90.
