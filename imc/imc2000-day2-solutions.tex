\section*{Problem 1}
(a) Show that the unit square can be partitioned into $n$ smaller squares for all large $n$.

(b) For $d\ge 2$, show there is $N(d)$ such that for $n\ge N(d)$ the unit $d$-cube partitions into $n$ smaller cubes.

\subsection*{Solution}
\textbf{Lemma.} If $\gcd(a,b)=1$, every sufficiently large $m$ is $ax+by$ with $x,y\ge 0$.

\textit{Proof.} $0,a,\dots,(b-1)a$ is a complete residue system mod $b$; choose $0\le x\le b-1$ with $ax\equiv m\pmod b$; then $y=(m-ax)/b\ge 0$ for $m\ge (b-1)a$.

Any dissection into $n$ cubes can be refined to $n+(a^d-1)$ by subdividing one cube into $a^d$. It suffices to show two coprime values of $a^d-1$. For $d=2$: $2^2-1=3$ and $3^2-1=8$ are coprime. In general, $2^d-1$ and $(2^d-1)^d-1$ are coprime.

\section*{Problem 2}
Let $f$ be continuous and nowhere monotone on $[0,1]$. Show local minima are dense.

\subsection*{Solution}
Let $(x-\alpha, x+\alpha)\subset[0,1]$. $f$ is not monotone on $[x-\alpha,x]$ nor on $[x,x+\alpha]$, so $\exists\, p<q\le x\le r<s$ in the respective intervals with $f(p)>f(q)$ and $f(r)<f(s)$. By Weierstrass, $f$ attains min on $[p,s]$; the endpoints are not the min. So the min lies in the interior, a local minimum.

\section*{Problem 3}
Let $p(z)$ be a polynomial of degree $n\ge 1$. Prove there are at least $n+1$ complex $z$ with $p(z)\in\{0,1\}$.

\subsection*{Solution}
Let $\mu(q,c)$ be the multiplicity of $c$ as root of $q$. Let $S_0=p^{-1}(0)$, $S_1=p^{-1}(1)$. Then $\sum_{c\in S_0}\mu(p,c) = \sum_{c\in S_1}\mu(p-1,c) = n$, and $\sum_{c\in S_0\cup S_1}\mu(p',c) \le n-1$.

If $c\in S_0$, $\mu(p,c)-\mu(p',c)=1$ (likewise for $S_1$ with $p-1$). So
\[|S_0|+|S_1| = \sum_{S_0}(\mu(p,c)-\mu(p',c)) + \sum_{S_1}(\mu(p-1,c)-\mu(p',c)) \ge n+n-(n-1) = n+1.\]

\section*{Problem 4}
A degree-6 polynomial is tangent to a line at three points $A_1, A_2, A_3$, with $A_2$ between. Let $k=A_2A_3/A_1A_2$, $K=$ ratio of the two bounded areas.

(a) If $k=1$, the areas are equal.

(b) $\frac{2}{7}k^5 < K < \frac{7}{2}k^5$.

\subsection*{Solution}
Place $A_2$ at origin, line as $y=a_5 x$. Tangency at $\pm a$ (when $k=1$) forces the polynomial $y = a_0(x^2-a^2)^2 x^2 + a_5 x$. The areas integrate $a_0(x^2-a^2)^2 x^2$ over $[-a,0]$ and $[0,a]$; the integrand is even, giving equal areas.

(b) $a_0=1$ WLOG: $y=(x+a)^2(x-b)^2 x^2 + a_5 x$ with $b=ka$. Compute
\[\int_{-a}^0 (x+a)^2(x-b)^2 x^2\,dx = \frac{a^7}{210}(7k^2+7k+2),\]
\[\int_0^b (x+a)^2(x-b)^2 x^2\,dx = \frac{a^7}{210}(2k^2+7k+7).\]
So $K = k^5\cdot \frac{2k^2+7k+7}{7k^2+7k+2}$. The rational factor decreases from $7/2$ (at $k=0$) to $2/7$ (as $k\to\infty$), yielding the bounds.

\section*{Problem 5}
Find all $f:\mathbb{R}^+\to\mathbb{R}^+$ with $f(x)f(yf(x))=f(x+y)$.

\subsection*{Solution}
If $f(x_0)>1$ for some $x_0$: set $y=x_0/(f(x_0)-1)$, i.e., $x_0+y = yf(x_0)$, giving $f(x_0)\cdot f(yf(x_0))=f(yf(x_0))$, so $f(x_0)=1$, contradiction. Hence $f\le 1$, and substituting gives $f$ decreasing.

If $f(x_0)=1$, then $f(x_0+y)=f(y)$ for all $y$; by monotonicity $f\equiv 1$ (so $f(x)=1/(1+0\cdot x)$).

If $f<1$ strictly, $f$ is strictly decreasing, injective. Writing the equation in two ways leads to $x = (x+y(1-f(x)))f(yf(x))$. Setting $x=1$, $z=yf(1)$, $a=(1-f(1))/f(1)$, we get $f(z)=1/(1+az)$.

Answer: $f(x) = \frac{1}{1+ax}$ with $a\ge 0$. (Direct verification.)

\textit{Alternative.} $\frac{f(x+y)-f(x)}{y} = f^2(x)\cdot \frac{f(yf(x))-1}{yf(x)}$ gives $f'(x)=-a f^2(x)$, so $(1/f)'=a$, i.e., $f=1/(ax+b)$ with $b=1$, $a\ge 0$.

\section*{Problem 6}
For $m\times m$ real $A$, $e^A=\sum A^n/n!$. Prove or disprove: for all real polynomials $p$ and $m\times m$ real $A,B$, $p(e^{AB})$ is nilpotent iff $p(e^{BA})$ is.

\subsection*{Solution}
True. For any polynomial $q$ and matrix $X$, $q(e^X) = \sum c_n X^n$. Let
\[C = \sum_{n\ge 1} c_n (BA)^{n-1} B = \sum c_n B(AB)^{n-1}.\]
Then $q(e^{AB}) = c_0 I + AC$ and $q(e^{BA}) = c_0 I + CA$. Since $AC$ and $CA$ have the same characteristic polynomial $f$, both $q(e^{AB}), q(e^{BA})$ have characteristic polynomial $f(x-c_0)$.

If $p(e^{AB})^k = 0$, take $q=p^k$: then $q(e^{AB})=0$, so $\chi_{q(e^{AB})}(x)=x^m$, hence $\chi_{q(e^{BA})}(x)=x^m$, and by Cayley--Hamilton $q(e^{BA})^m=p(e^{BA})^{km}=0$.
