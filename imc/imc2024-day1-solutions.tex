\section*{Problem 1}
Determine all pairs $(a,b) \in \mathbb{C} \times \mathbb{C}$ satisfying $|a| = |b| = 1$ and $a + b + a\overline{b} \in \mathbb{R}$.

(proposed by Mike Daas, Universiteit Leiden)

\paragraph{Solution 1.}
Write $a = e^{ix}$ and $b = e^{iy}$ for some $x, y \in [0, 2\pi)$. Using Euler's formula and standard identities, we get
\begin{align*}
\operatorname{Im}(a + b + a\overline{b}) &= (\sin x + \sin y) + \sin(x - y) \\
&= 2\sin\frac{x+y}{2}\cos\frac{x-y}{2} + 2\sin\frac{x-y}{2}\cos\frac{x-y}{2} \\
&= 4\sin\frac{x}{2}\cos\frac{y}{2}\cos\frac{x-y}{2}.
\end{align*}
Hence $a + b + a\overline{b}$ is real iff $\sin\frac{x}{2} = 0$, $\cos\frac{y}{2} = 0$, or $\cos\frac{x-y}{2} = 0$, corresponding to $x = 2k\pi$, $y = (2k+1)\pi$, $x = y + (2k+1)\pi$. Therefore the solutions are
\[
(1, b), \quad (a, -1), \quad (a, -a) \quad \text{with } |a| = |b| = 1.
\]

\paragraph{Solution 2.}
Notice $a + b + a\overline{b} \in \mathbb{R} \iff 1 + a + b + a\overline{b} \in \mathbb{R}$. Let $c \in \mathbb{C}$ be such that $a = c^2$. Then
\[
\overline{c}(1 + a + b + a\overline{b}) = \overline{c} + c + \overline{c}b + c\overline{b} \in \mathbb{R}.
\]
So either $c \in \mathbb{R}$ (giving $a = 1$), or $1 + a + b + a\overline{b} = 0$, which factors as $(a + \overline{b})(1 + b) \cdot$ \textit{(after multiplying through)}, giving $a = -\overline{b}$ (i.e.\ $a = -a$ when $|b|=1$) or $b = -1$.

\section*{Problem 2}
Find $\lim_{n \to \infty} S_n$ where $S_n = \log\sqrt[n^2]{1^1 \cdot 2^2 \cdots n^n} - \log\sqrt{n}$.

(proposed by Sergey Chernov, Belarusian State University, Minsk)

\paragraph{Solution.}
\begin{align*}
S_n &= \frac{1}{n^2}\sum_{k=1}^n k \log k - \frac{1}{2}\log n \\
&= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=1}^n \frac{k}{n}\left(\log\frac{k}{n} + \log n\right) - \frac{1}{2}\log n \\
&= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=1}^n \frac{k}{n}\log\frac{k}{n} + \frac{\log n}{2n}.
\end{align*}
The second term tends to $0$. The first is a Riemann sum for $\int_0^1 x \log x\, dx = \left[\frac{x^2}{2}\log x - \frac{x^2}{4}\right]_0^1 = -\frac{1}{4}$. So $\lim S_n = -\frac{1}{4}$.

\section*{Problem 3}
For which positive integers $n$ does there exist an $n \times n$ matrix $A$ with entries in $\{0,1\}$ such that $A^2 = J_n$ (matrix of all ones)?

(proposed by Alex Avdiushenko, Neapolis University Paphos, Cyprus)

\paragraph{Solution.}
\textbf{Answer:} iff $n$ is a perfect square.

From $A^3 = A J_n = J_n A$, all row sums and column sums of $A$ equal some $k$, so $A J_n = k J_n$. Then $n J_n = J_n^2 = A(A J_n) = k^2 J_n$, so $n = k^2$.

Construction for $n = k^2$: for $\ell = 0, \ldots, k-1$, let $B_\ell$ be the cyclic-shift permutation matrix ($(i,j)$-entry is $1$ iff $j - i \equiv \ell \pmod k$). Set
\[
A = \begin{pmatrix} B_0 & B_1 & \cdots & B_{k-1} \\ B_0 & B_1 & \cdots & B_{k-1} \\ \vdots & & & \vdots \\ B_0 & B_1 & \cdots & B_{k-1} \end{pmatrix}.
\]
The $(i,j)$th block of $A^2$ is $(B_0 + \cdots + B_{k-1}) B_{j-1} = J_k B_{j-1} = J_k$, so $A^2 = J_{k^2}$.

\section*{Problem 4}
(Setup as in problem.) Prove $H$ does not depend on $w$.

(proposed by Ivan Mitrofanov, Saarland University)

\paragraph{Solution.}
Let $X_m$ denote the set of products $g_1 \ldots g_m$ with each $g_i \in \{g, h\}$.

\textbf{Lemma.} For all $j = 1, \ldots, n$ and all $a, b \in X_j$, the ratio $a^{-1} b \in H$.

\emph{Proof by induction on $j$.}

\emph{Base case $j = 1$:} By pigeonhole, there exist $k < \ell$ with $(w_{k+1}, \ldots, w_{k+n-1}) = (w_{\ell+1}, \ldots, w_{\ell+n-1})$. Since $w$ is not eventually periodic, there exists $m > 0$ with $w_{k+m} \neq w_{\ell+m}$; we have $m \geq n$, so $w_{k+m-i} = w_{\ell+m-i}$ for $1 \leq i \leq n-1$. The products $x = w_{k+m-n+1} \ldots w_{k+m}$ and $y = w_{\ell+m-n+1} \ldots w_{\ell+m}$ both lie in $H$, so their ratios (which are $g^{-1}h$ and $h^{-1}g$) are in $H$.

\emph{Induction step $j-1 \to j$:} Call $a \in X_j$ a $g$-element if $a = g a_1$ with $a_1 \in X_{j-1}$, similarly for $h$-element. Ratios of two $g$-elements (or two $h$-elements) are in $H$ by induction. It remains to find a $g$-element and $h$-element whose ratio is in $H$.

With $k, \ell, m$ as above, the products
\begin{align*}
v &= w_{k+m-n+j} \ldots w_{k+m+j-1}, \\
u &= w_{\ell+m-n+j} \ldots w_{\ell+m+j-1}
\end{align*}
lie in $H$, and $u^{-1}v$ is a ratio of a $g$-element and an $h$-element (since $\{w_{k+m}, w_{\ell+m}\} = \{g,h\}$).

Applying the lemma for $j = n$, $H$ is generated by $X_n$, independent of $w$.

\section*{Problem 5}
(Setup as in problem.) Show $f(p) \geq f(q)$ when $p$ is closer to origin than $q$.

(proposed by Fedor Petrov, St Petersburg State University)

\paragraph{Solution.}
By radial symmetry, $f$ depends only on distance from origin $o$; WLOG $p$ lies on segment $\overline{oq}$. For $x \in B$ let $f_x(x_1,\ldots,x_n)$ be the indicator that $x \in \mathrm{conv}(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$. It suffices to prove the deterministic inequality
\begin{equation}
\sum f_p(\pm x_1, \ldots, \pm x_n) \geq \sum f_q(\pm x_1, \ldots, \pm x_n), \tag{1}
\end{equation}
where summation is over all $2^n$ sign choices and points are in general position.

Use the formula: for a simplicial convex polyhedron $P$ with facets $P_1, \ldots, P_k$, setting $Q_i = \mathrm{conv}(o, P_i)$, we have $\chi_P = \sum \pm \chi_{Q_i}$ (sign $+$ if $o$ and $P$ are on same side of $P_i$, else $-$).

Applying to each of the $2^n$ polyhedra with vertices $\pm x_1, \ldots, \pm x_n$ and summing, $\sum_P \chi_P = \sum \alpha_S \chi_S$ where $S$ ranges over simplices formed by $o$ and $d$ points from $\{\pm x_i\}$ (no $\pm$ pair).

Fix such $S$ with vertices $o, x_1, \ldots, x_d$. The hyperplane through $x_1, \ldots, x_d$ splits $\mathbb{R}^d$ into $H^+$ (containing $o$) and $H^-$. For each pair $\{x_i, -x_i\}$ ($i > d$), either both lie in $H^+$, or one in each. So the coefficient $\alpha_S$ counts contributions: $+$ when other vertices are from $H^+$, $-$ when from $H^-$. Thus $\alpha_S \geq 0$, giving (1).
